Answers to the most common questions from automotive and machinery exhibitors — drawn from 25+ years of moving heavy industrial equipment to the world's leading shows.

How do you ship a large CNC machine to a trade show in Germany?

Shipping a CNC machining centre to Germany requires careful engineering at every stage. We begin with a cargo survey — measuring the machine's dimensions, recording its weight and centre of gravity, and identifying crane lift points. The machine is then prepared: spindles locked, axes secured, coolant drained, and all protruding elements removed or padded. We build a custom engineered timber crate with anti-vibration isolation mounts, apply VCI film inside to prevent corrosion, and attach shock-watch indicators. For transit, we select sea freight via flat-rack or standard 20/40ft container depending on size, booking with a carrier that maintains tilting restrictions. For shows like EMO or Hannover Messe, we co-ordinate arrival timing to align with the venue's move-in schedule and book the official venue handler's cranes in advance — because both Deutsche Messe and Messe München require all heavy machinery placement to be conducted by their appointed lifting contractor.

What is out-of-gauge (OOG) cargo and how do you handle it?

Out-of-gauge cargo is any shipment that exceeds the internal dimensions of a standard ISO container — typically over 5.9m long, 2.3m wide, or 2.3m tall. For exhibition machinery, this commonly applies to construction equipment, large agricultural machines, industrial presses, and turbines. We handle OOG cargo using flat-rack containers (where cargo is secured to an open platform), open-top containers (for tall items that can be covered with a tarpaulin), or RoRo (roll-on roll-off) vessels where machines can be driven or towed aboard. On arrival at the destination port, OOG cargo requires specialist port handling and, for road transport to the venue, an abnormal load permit from the relevant road authority. In some cases a police escort is required. We survey the route in advance, obtain all permits, and co-ordinate the escort. At the venue, we arrange crane-offload with the approved venue handler.

Does exhibit machinery need to be declared as dangerous goods?

In many cases, yes. Machinery with residual fuel in tanks, charged lithium-ion batteries, hydraulic oil in closed systems, compressed gas cylinders, or capacitor-charged electrical systems must be declared and documented as dangerous goods under IMDG (sea) or IATA (air) regulations. Failure to comply can result in cargo refusal by the carrier, significant fines, or delays at customs. STOP India's team includes IMDG/IATA dangerous goods certified professionals who assess each machine before shipment. Where DG declaration is required, we prepare the full DG documentation, ensure packaging meets the relevant packing instructions, and select carriers with the appropriate DG acceptance authorisation. For machines that are being drained and certified clean prior to shipment, we can assist with the documentation of that process to support a non-DG declaration.

How does an ATA Carnet work for machinery exhibits and what happens on return?

An ATA Carnet is an international customs document that allows goods to be temporarily exported to and imported from participating countries without paying import duties or taxes — provided the goods are returned within the carnet's validity period (usually one year). For machinery exhibits, the carnet is obtained from an authorised issuing body in India (such as FIEO) and must list every item being exported with precise descriptions, values, and quantities. At each border crossing — export from India, import at destination, and re-export on return — the carnet is presented and counterfoils are stamped. STOP India prepares the carnet application, manages all border presentations during the trip, monitors re-export deadlines, and manages the return importation to India — ensuring the carnet is fully discharged and no bond forfeiture is triggered.

What documentation is required to export industrial machinery from India?

Exporting industrial machinery from India requires a commercial invoice, packing list, a shipping bill filed with Indian Customs, an IEC (Importer-Exporter Code), and — depending on the machinery — potentially a DGFT export licence or SCOMET clearance for controlled goods. Some categories of machinery also require a BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards) certification or a technical specifications document. For machinery being exported temporarily for exhibition, an ATA Carnet or a provisional export bond is used in lieu of standard export payment terms. STOP India prepares a full documentation checklist specific to your machinery category, files all customs documents, and ensures destination import documentation is simultaneously prepared — so there are no gaps in the chain at either end.

Can you manage the entire exhibit — machines, stand materials, and marketing collateral — in one shipment?

Yes — and this is usually the most efficient and cost-effective approach. A consolidated shipment that combines your exhibit machines, stand structures, display boards, promotional literature, branded merchandise, and spare parts into a single managed consignment simplifies customs documentation, reduces total freight cost through consolidation, and means one supervisor receives everything at the venue. We are specialists in mixed industrial consignments — handling the OOG machines, standard cartons, and custom crates within the same planned shipment, each packed and documented correctly. Our on-site team receives the full consignment, co-ordinates the crane and forklift placement of machines, and ensures your stand is fully built and your materials unpacked before the show opens.
